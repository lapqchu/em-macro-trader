**Source**: Brent Donnelly, *Alpha Trader* (2021)
**Derived from**: donnelly-alpha-trader-digest.md
**Relationship**: Extends Art of Currency Trading checklists with deeper psychology, methodology, and mathematics layer

## Checklist 1: Narrative Cycle Assessment

Use when evaluating *where* a market theme or trade idea sits in its lifecycle. Determines timing — the difference between a good view and a good trade.

| # | Question | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Can you name the dominant narrative?** | If you can't articulate the story in one sentence, you don't understand it well enough to trade it |
| 2 | **How widely known is this narrative?** | Under the Radar (few aware) → Momentum Building (professionals know) → Primary Trend (everyone knows) → Final Hype (non-experts joining) |
| 3 | **Is news reinforcing or challenging the narrative?** | Reinforcing = Stages 2–3; First contradictions = Stage 4; Narrative changing but price holding = Stage 6 (BEST SETUP) |
| 4 | **Who is buying/selling now?** | Professionals only = early; Retail joining = late; Non-experts talking about it at dinner = very late |
| 5 | **Has the narrative turned but price hasn't?** | YES = Stage 6 — the most profitable moment. Enter in the direction of the new narrative. |
| 6 | **How long has this narrative been running?** | Weeks = potentially early; Months = primary trend; Quarters = late stage |
| 7 | **What timeframe am I operating on?** | Narrative cycles run on multiple fractals (24hr to multi-month). Identify which fractal you're trading. |

**Stage → Action Matrix:**

| Stage | Action | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (Under the Radar) | Small position; wide stop; high reward if right | May wait a long time for catalyst |
| 2 (Momentum Builds) | Enter with trend; add on pullbacks | Early pullbacks can be sharp |
| 3 (Primary Trend) | Ride; tighten stops but don't exit prematurely — "well-known" ≠ exhausted | Complacency risk |
| 4 (First Cracks) | Reduce; tighten stops significantly; watch for Stage 5 squeeze | Shake-outs that reverse |
| 5 (Final Hype) | Take profit; do NOT initiate fresh longs into FOMO | Most volatile, most surprising; choppy |
| 6 (Peak and Turn) | **BEST SETUP**: Initiate position in direction of NEW narrative | Requires conviction to go against price |
| 7 (The End) | Short if still running; avoid initiating longs | Theme is over; move on |

**Cross-reference**: Combine with Booth's meme lifecycle analysis for structural themes. Narrative cycle applies to both tactical (days–weeks) and strategic (months–quarters) horizons.

## Checklist 2: Type I / II / III Conviction Grading

Use to determine position size based on conviction level. Evolved from Art of Currency Trading's 3/4/5-star system with explicit formulas.

| # | Question | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Does this idea pass the 7-step trade lifecycle?** | Must pass filtering (Checklist 3) before grading conviction |
| 2 | **How many of the five filtering dimensions align?** | Narrative cycle + positioning + events + correlated markets + technicals |
| 3 | **Is this a routine setup or a major narrative shift?** | Routine = Type I; Major shift = Type II; Watershed = Type III |
| 4 | **How often do setups like this appear?** | Monthly+ = Type I; 4–5 per year = Type II; 1–2 per career = Type III |
| 5 | **What is my YTD P&L?** | Critical input for Type II/III sizing formula |
| 6 | **Am I in a position of strength?** | If down on year or near stop loss → even Type III stays small |

**Sizing Rules:**

| Type | Description | Risk Formula |
|---|---|---|
| **Type I** (Run of the Mill) | Standard trade, regular process | 1–3% of free capital |
| **Type II** (High Conviction) | Major narrative shift, significant dislocation; 4–5/year | **3% of free capital + 10% of YTD P&L** |
| **Type III** (Outlier Opportunity) | Watershed moment; career-defining | Up to 50% of YTD P&L (only if in strong position) |

**Monthly Stop Loss Scale** (adapt to your own limits):

| YTD P&L Position | Monthly Stop (% of full-year stop) |
|---|---|
| Negative | 5% (minimal risk, preserve capital) |
| Breakeven to +25% of target | 10% |
| +25% to +50% of target | 15% |
| +50% to +75% of target | 20% |
| +75% to target | 25% |
| Above target | 30%+ (play with house money) |

**Key insight**: Risk increases *exponentially*, not linearly, with conviction. The Type II formula creates a call-option P&L structure — similar downside in bad years, dramatically better upside in good years.

**Cross-reference**: Maps to Art of Currency Trading 3/4/5-star system. Type I ≈ 3-star (1%). Type II ≈ 4–5-star with YTD P&L accelerator. Type III has no equivalent — reserved for true outlier moments.

## Checklist 3: Trade Filtering (Five Dimensions + Alternative Hypotheses)

Use BEFORE entering any trade. This is the quality control gate between idea and execution.

| # | Dimension | Question | Pass / Fail Criteria |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Narrative position** | Where is this idea in the narrative cycle? (Checklist 1) | Stage 1–3 or Stage 6 = Pass. Stage 5 or 7 = Fail (fading or dead). |
| 2 | **Positioning** | Does market positioning strengthen or weaken the idea? | Crowded in your direction = weak; Crowded against you + catalyst = strong |
| 3 | **Event calendar** | Do upcoming events support or threaten the trade? | Major opposing event within holding period with unclear outcome = caution |
| 4 | **Correlated markets** | Do cross-market signals confirm or contradict? | ≥2 correlated markets confirming = Pass. Multiple contradictions = Fail. |
| 5 | **Technical setup** | Good entry, clear stop, reasonable R:R? | Identifiable stop, R:R ≥ 2:1, entry near a Seven Deadly Setup = Pass |

### Alternative Hypotheses Gate (ACH-Lite)

Before proceeding past filtering, answer these three questions:

| # | Question | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 6 | **Pre-mortem**: If this trade lost money, what was the most likely reason? | Forces you to articulate the bear case before committing capital |
| 7 | **Strong opinions, weakly held**: What evidence would make you abandon this trade? | Defines falsification criteria ex-ante |
| 8 | **Peer check**: If you described this trade to a respected peer, what would they push back on? | Surfaces blind spots through perspective-taking |

**Minimum to proceed**: ≥3 of 5 dimensions passing + satisfactory answers to all three alternative hypothesis questions.

**Cross-reference**: This extends the Art of Currency Trading's Five-Star qualification with the narrative cycle layer and formal alternative hypothesis requirement.

## Checklist 4: Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH)

Use for high-conviction trades (Type II/III) or when you find yourself strongly committed to a single view. Adapted from CIA's Heuer methodology.

| # | Step | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **List hypotheses** | Write down ≥3 possible explanations for what is happening (not just your view and "the opposite"). Seek genuinely diverse perspectives. |
| 2 | **List evidence** | Enumerate all relevant evidence: data points, price action, positioning, CB communication, flows, political developments. |
| 3 | **Build matrix** | Create a hypotheses × evidence grid. Mark each cell: Consistent (C), Inconsistent (I), or Not Applicable (N/A). |
| 4 | **Assess diagnosticity** | Which evidence items actually distinguish between hypotheses? Evidence consistent with ALL hypotheses has zero diagnostic value — delete it. |
| 5 | **Focus on disconfirmation** | Try to DISPROVE each hypothesis, not prove your preferred one. The hypothesis that survives the most disconfirmation attempts is strongest. |
| 6 | **Sensitivity analysis** | What if your most important piece of evidence is wrong? Does your conclusion survive? |
| 7 | **Report relative likelihood** | Don't just pick a winner — assign rough probabilities to ALL surviving hypotheses. |
| 8 | **Set tripwires** | Define specific observable outcomes that would shift probabilities meaningfully. These become your reassessment triggers. |

**When to use full ACH vs. ACH-Lite (Checklist 3, items 6–8)**:
- ACH-Lite: Standard Type I trades, daily assessment
- Full ACH: Type II/III trades, when you notice confirmation bias, when stakes are high, when the market is confusing you

**Cross-reference**: Extends Checklist 3's alternative hypotheses gate into a full analytical framework. Tripwires from Step 8 feed directly into the trade lifecycle's reassessment triggers.

## Checklist 5: Risk Appetite Self-Scoring

Use weekly (ideally Monday morning) and after any significant P&L event.

### Part A: Score Your Risk Appetite (1–10)

| Score | Profile | Key Risk |
|---|---|---|
| 1–4 | Too risk-averse | Missing opportunities; under-trading |
| 5–6 | Conservative | Good in volatile environments; may lag in trending markets |
| 7–7.5 | **Optimal zone** | Natural balance of risk-taking and discipline |
| 8–9 | Aggressive | Needs strict loss limits and cooling-off rules |
| 10 | Sensation-seeking | Honestly evaluate: professional or gambler? |

### Part B: Check for Distorting Effects

| # | Effect | Signal | Countermeasure |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Zero-bound effect** | P&L approaching zero/negative → becoming too cautious | Set MINIMUM trade size; use trade quotas; remind yourself a drawdown is the worst time to stop trading |
| 2 | **End-of-period effect** | Year-end approaching → reducing risk prematurely | Markets don't know your calendar; maintain process |
| 3 | **House money effect** | After big gains → trading recklessly, sizing up without better ideas | Let gains settle psychologically before increasing risk; the "it's just house money" feeling is when you give it back |

### Part C: Prescriptions by Score

| If your score is... | Do this |
|---|---|
| Too low (1–4) | Set minimum trade sizes. Force yourself to execute trade quotas. Don't exit before triggers fire. |
| Moderate (5–7) | When at high-water mark, pre-define an "aggressive risk amount" (e.g., up $6M → risk $1M aggressively). |
| Optimal (7–8) | Maintain. Check for drift monthly. |
| Too high (8–9) | Set strict max daily/monthly/yearly loss limits. Mandatory cooling-off after hitting any limit. |
| Way too high (10) | Get honest with yourself. Consider DSM-V gambling criteria. May need external accountability. |

## Checklist 6: Variance vs. Bad Process Diagnostic

Use after any losing streak (≥5 days or ≥2 consecutive weekly losses). The critical question: am I losing because of variance or because my process is broken?

### Part A: Is This Variance? (Acceptable)

| # | Diagnostic | If Yes → |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Was I wrong a lot? | Being wrong is part of trading. Check: were the ideas well-reasoned and well-sized? If yes → variance. |
| 2 | Did I get unlucky? (Bad news, stopped out at extremes, gap risk) | Luck is real and mean-reverting. If the process was sound → variance. Wait it out. |

### Part B: Is This Bad Process? (Fix Immediately)

| # | Diagnostic | If Yes → |
|---|---|---|
| 3 | Am I reading the market well but losing money? | **Process breakdown.** Execution, sizing, or timing is failing even though the analysis is correct. Review entries and exits specifically. |
| 4 | Do I have a plan for each trade? | **No plan = not a trade.** Write stops, targets, and reassessment triggers before entering. |
| 5 | Am I overtrading? | Count trades. If more than your historical average → cut back. Use time windows or trade quotas. |
| 6 | Did I leave stale orders in the market? | **Poor attention.** Review all open orders daily. Cancel anything that no longer reflects your current view. |
| 7 | Am I moving stops, averaging losers, or ignoring plan? | **Discipline failure.** This is the most dangerous category. Go to 20% size until discipline returns. |
| 8 | Has the market regime changed and I haven't adapted? | **Adaptation failure.** Check: are your strategies regime-appropriate? (Trend strategies in range, mean-reversion in trend = guaranteed losses.) Update your three daily variables (vol, liquidity, trend/range). |
| 9 | Am I under external pressure (manager, P&L stress, personal problems)? | **Environmental failure.** Reduce risk; address the external problem first. Trading while emotionally compromised compounds losses. |

### Verdict Protocol

- If Part A only → maintain process, reduce size slightly if needed, and wait for variance to normalize
- If any Part B item is "yes" → STOP. Fix the process issue BEFORE resuming normal trading
- If multiple Part B items → initiate full slump protocol (Art of Currency Trading Checklist 8)

**Cross-reference**: Extends Art of Currency Trading's slump detection with a diagnostic layer that distinguishes variance from process failure — preventing both premature methodology abandonment during bad runs and persistence with broken process during genuine failure.

## Checklist 7: Crisis Market Adaptation (VIX > 40)

Use when VIX crosses 40 (only 2.2% of trading days since 1990). Normal rules are suspended. This is a SEPARATE operating mode.

### Entry Criteria

| # | Check | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Is this a true crisis or routine risk-off?** | VIX > 40 AND credit markets stressed AND cross-asset correlation spiking → true crisis. VIX spike alone on a single event ≠ crisis. |
| 2 | **What kind of crisis?** | Liquidity crisis (flash crash, margin call cascade) vs. fundamental crisis (recession, sovereign default, pandemic) → different playbooks |

### Four Crisis Rules

| # | Rule | Implementation |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Position size is everything** | Use forward-looking vol (1-week implied from options) to size. If unavailable, use average daily range over past 5/10/20 days. Reduce normal size by 60–85%. Stop floors: day traders ≥ 1/3 of ADR; swing traders ≥ 1 full ADR. Smaller position + wider stop. |
| 2 | **Keep an open mind** | Do NOT assume "it's already moved enough." Oil went from $65 to $50 to $27 to MINUS $40 in COVID. **Fading big moves in a crisis = recipe for ruin.** |
| 3 | **Overbought/oversold is useless** | Put/call ratios, Greed & Fear, DSI, Deviation — ALL useless during real crises. Differentiate between routine risk-off (where these work) and true crisis (where they don't). |
| 4 | **Have courage** | Don't hide. The best alpha traders make their years/careers during crises. It is better to try and fail than to sit paralyzed while peers extract outsized P&L. |

### Position Sizing in Crisis (Illustrative)

| Normal Position | Crisis Position (VIX > 40) | Stop Width |
|---|---|---|
| $20M EURUSD | $3–5M EURUSD | 2–3× normal stop width |
| 100 S&P contracts | 15–25 contracts | 2× ADR away minimum |
| $10M EM NDF | $1.5–2.5M EM NDF | 3–4× normal (EM vol explodes) |

**Cross-reference**: Crisis markets override Willer's normal tactical framework (rate cycle, factor signals). During true crises, focus on: (1) global liquidity conditions, (2) CB response function speed, (3) position sizing discipline, (4) cross-asset correlation (everything trades together). Booth's blow-up indicators are the early warning system for EM-specific crises.

## Checklist 8: Strategy Decay Detection

Use quarterly to evaluate whether your active strategies still have edge. Strategy decay is slow and insidious — by the time you notice, you've given back months of P&L.

| # | Diagnostic | Warning Sign |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Rolling 6M P&L of this strategy** | Declining despite consistent application. Compare current 6M to peak 6M and to trailing 2-year average. |
| 2 | **Win rate trend** | Falling win rate on a strategy that previously had stable win rate = crowding or regime shift. |
| 3 | **Who else is doing this?** | If your strategy is now discussed in mainstream media, available via cheap APIs, or replicable in Excel → likely crowded. |
| 4 | **Has technology democratized the edge?** | What cost $500K in infrastructure 5 years ago may now be free. HFT arbitrages are the canonical example. |
| 5 | **Has the market structure changed?** | New regulation, new products, new participants, decimalization-style changes can kill strategies overnight. |
| 6 | **Am I being honest or sentimental?** | "Kill your darlings" — don't persist with a dead strategy because of emotional attachment to past success. |

### Decision Protocol

| Assessment | Action |
|---|---|
| P&L declining but regime is unusual → may recover | Reduce size to 50%; set 3-month review date; define specific recovery criteria |
| P&L declining AND crowding evidence | Reduce to 25% or cut entirely; explore adaptation (e.g., Donnelly's lead/lag: assume it WON'T work, then look for exceptions) |
| P&L negative for 2+ rolling periods despite sound process | Kill the strategy. Grieve briefly. Move on. The market has evolved past this edge. |
| Strategy still works but less well | Accept lower returns; reduce allocation; diversify edge sources |

**Key principle**: Your default assumption should be that ANY strategy will eventually decay. The question is when, not if. The Red Queen effect applies: you must continuously develop new edges just to maintain the same place.

## Checklist 9: Self-Management Systems

Use daily. These are the operational habits that separate consistent performers from traders who have occasional good quarters.

### Part A: Daily Sheet (Morning Prep)

| # | Item | How to Assess |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Mood score (1–10)** | Honest self-assessment. Include physical state (sleep, exercise, health). |
| 2 | **Opportunity score (1–10)** | How rich is the current trading environment? High vol + clear narratives + good setups = high. Dead quiet + no catalysts = low. |
| 3 | **Risk budget** | Formula: (avg of mood + opportunity scores) / 10 × max daily risk. Low combined score → small risk budget. |
| 4 | **Key technical levels** | S/R levels for your active markets; where MAs are; any Seven Deadly Setups forming. |
| 5 | **Today's plan** | Specific trades with entry, stop, target. "I'll play it by ear" is not a plan. |
| 6 | **Personal reminders** | Your mantra, your One Goal, anything you're working on from journal review. |
| 7 | **"Never schedule meetings before noon"** | Protect your best trading hours. Morning overlap is when alpha is generated. |

### Part B: 12-12 Journaling System

Email a trusted peer DAILY with:

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| **12 hours back** | Summary of what happened in markets, what you did, P&L, any mistakes |
| **12 hours forward** | Plan with specific trades, stops, risk levels. "What am I going to do tomorrow?" |

**Why this works**: Forces accountability, habit formation, real-time feedback. The peer doesn't need to respond — the act of writing and sending creates the discipline.

### Part C: One Goal System

| # | Step | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | After monthly journal review, set ONE short-term goal | Not 5–15 goals. ONE. Focus compounds. |
| 2 | Goal must be specific and achievable in a few months | "Reduce overtrading" is too vague. "Maximum 3 trades per day for the next 8 weeks" is specific. |
| 3 | Review at each monthly journal session | Is the goal still relevant? Have you achieved it? If achieved → set a new one. |

### Part D: Default Mode Network (DMN) Awareness

| # | Question | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **What do you do when idle?** | Positive: scanning charts, generating ideas, creative thinking. Negative: urge to trade, checking phone at 3am, browsing positions aimlessly. |
| 2 | **Can you hold contradictory skills?** | Alpha traders: sit and do nothing for long periods AND act aggressively without hesitation. If you can only do one → identify which and work on the other. |

### Part E: Emotion Management Protocol

| Emotional State | Action |
|---|---|
| Euphoric after big win | **Cut position or take full profit.** Euphoria → overconfidence → give-back. Time delay: go for a walk. |
| Angry / frustrated after loss | **Cut to half size, widen stop.** Time delay: step away until physiological markers (face flushing, heart rate) normalize. |
| Anxious / fearful | **Reduce size until comfortable.** Ask: am I sized wrong, or is the trade wrong? |
| Bored / understimulated | **Do NOT trade to fill the void.** Boredom trades are negative EV. Read, research, exercise instead. |
| Invincible / manic | **Square up. Get flat. Reset.** This is the most dangerous state. It precedes the worst losses. |

**Key insight**: Time delay is the simplest and most effective strategy. Full-blown emotions dissipate naturally. The Donnelly Flash Crash case study: he recognized emotional flooding → physically stepped away → let System 2 re-engage → made a rational recovery trade worth $2.8M. The recovery was only possible because he stopped.

## Checklist 10: Specific Patterns and Heuristics

Quick-reference for tradeable patterns with empirical backing from Alpha Trader.

| Pattern | Setup | Data | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Turnaround Tuesday** | Stocks fall Thu+Fri, then drop >5% on Monday | Avg Tuesday return: +4.3% (since 1960) vs. +0.9% unconditional | Buy into Monday close or Tuesday open; stop below Monday low |
| **September Seasonal** | US equities weak Sept 16 – Oct 9 | Worst 5-day window = week after Sept opex | Reduce long equity exposure or buy puts into mid-September |
| **Bubble Deflation** | Asset crashes from all-time high | BTC 2017: -84.2%, NASDAQ 2000: -83.6%, Nikkei 1989: -82.0% | Look for entry near 80–85% drawdown from the high |
| **Month-End Dollar** | US equities up big during the month | European pension funds sell USD into 4pm London fix on last business day | Position for USD weakness into month-end London fix |
| **Bankrupt Stock Reopen** | Stock halted for bankruptcy; trading resumes | Shorts take profit sub-$1; buying reopen = profitable 1-day to 1-week | Buy at reopen; short-term trade only |
| **Arcsine Law** | Period highs/lows cluster at START and END | U-shaped distribution, not random | Don't assume a mid-period extreme is "the move" — expect more at period boundaries |
| **"Everybody's Bearish, Nobody's Short"** | Strong consensus + no positioning | Impulsive move once event passes | Enter in consensus direction AFTER the obvious risk event clears — positioning vacuum creates momentum |
| **Run-Up Trade** | Predictable price action in days before major events | Positioning reduction + obvious trade + lotto ticket | Position 2–3 days before the event in the direction of the obvious trade |

**Cross-reference**: These patterns complement the Art of Currency Trading's Seven Deadly Setups. The setups are technical (entry/stop/target); these patterns are behavioral/structural (timing and market microstructure).
